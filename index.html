How the timing calculator works

The goal is simple: show you when your evenings are setting you up for either a stable or chaotic morning, so it feels less random.

  1. Enter last night. Log when you ate dinner or late snacks, when you stopped liquids, when you took magnesium, and when you actually slept and woke up.
  2. Add context. Add how sugary the evening was, whether you used electrolytes, and rough levels of fiber, stress, and exercise.
  3. Get a Morning Stability Score. The tool gives a score from 0 to 100 plus a breakdown of what helped or hurt your odds of a calm morning. Then you log how your gut actually behaved.
You are not being judged. You are just looking at a timing model for IBS and visceral hypersensitivity instead of guessing every morning.

Why timing can matter more than “what you ate”

Most IBS advice is a food list. For a lot of sensitive people there is a different problem: the timing and the nervous system. If you have visceral hypersensitivity, your system may react not just to what you ate, but to when your gut has to move water, magnesium, sugar, and waste through a sensitive colon relative to your sleep and wake cycle.

When evenings drift late or stack badly, you compress a lot of gut work into the hours when you are trying to wake up. That is when mornings tend to blow up.

Example timing pattern that often goes wrong

  • Drinking liquids right up until bed or waking to drink several times.
  • Taking magnesium very close to sleep instead of a consistent 60 to 90 minute window.
  • Sleep start drifting later and later with less than 7 hours in bed.
  • Wake time still early, so the gut has less time to settle before you stand up.

For many people with IBS or visceral hypersensitivity, that combination creates an urgency window in the first hour or two after waking. The calculator turns that window into something you can see instead of just dread.
